1. Propositional Logic

Logic forms the bedrock of mathematical reasoning. It provides the rules and tools to determine the truth or falsehood of statements and to construct valid arguments.

1.1 Statements and Truth Values

A statement is a declarative sentece that is unequivocally either true (T) or false (F).

Examples of Statements:

  • A: "4 is an even number." (T)
  • B: "15 is divisible by 7". (F)
  • C: "The square root of 2 is irrational." (T)

Sentences like "Let's go!" or "What is your name?" are not statements because they cannot be assigned a truth value.

1.2 Logical Operators

Simple statements can be combined using logical operators to form compound statements. The truth value of a compound statement depends entirely on the truth values of its components and the connective used. This is systematically analyzed using truth tables.

Primary operators:

  • \(¬\) (NOT): Negation. It reverses the truth value.
  • \(∧\) (AND): Conjunction. \(A ∧ B\) is true only if both A and B are true.
  • \(∨\) (OR): Disjunction. \(A ∨ B\) is true if at least one of A or B is true. This is the "inclusive or".
  • \(⇒\) (IMPLIES): \(A ⇒ B\) means "if A, then B".
    It is false only when A is true and B is false.
    If A is false, the implication is considered true by convention ("from falsehood, anything follows").
  • \(⇔\) (IF AND ONLY IF): Equivalence. \(A ⇔ B\) is true when A and B have the same truth value.

1.3 Tautologies, Paradoxes and Equivalence

Some compound statements are special because their truth value is independent of their components.

  • A Tautology is a statement that is always true.
    Example: \(¬A ∨ A\) (The Law of the Excluded Middle)
  • A Paradox (or Contradiction) is a statement that is always false.
    Example: \(¬A ∧ A\)

Two statements are logically equivalent (\(A⇔B\)) if they have identical truth tables.
If \(A\) and \(B\) are equivalent, then \(A⇔B\) is a tautology.

1.4 Fundamental Logical Rules

Several equivalence rules allow us to simplify and manipulate logical expressions.

  • Commutativity: \(A ∧ B ⇔ B ∧ A\) and \(A ∨ B ⇔ B ∨ A\)
  • Associativity: \((A ∧ B) ∧ C ⇔ A ∧ (B ∧ C)\) and \((A ∨ B) ∨ C ⇔ A ∨ (B ∨ C)\)
  • Distributivity: \(A ∧ (B ∨ C) ⇔ (A ∧ B) ∨ (A ∧ C)\) and \(A ∨ (B ∧ C) ⇔ (A ∨ B) ∧ (A ∨ C)\)
  • De Morgan's Laws:
    • \(¬(A ∧ B) ⇔ ¬A ∨ ¬B\)
    • \(¬(A ∨ B) ⇔ ¬A ∧ ¬B\)
  • Double Negation: \(¬(¬A) ⇔ A\)
  • Implication: \((A ⇒ B) ⇔ (¬A ∨ B)\)
  • Contraposition: (\(A⇒ B) ⇔ (¬B ⇒ ¬A)\)

1.5 Crucial Implications and Proof Techniques

The relationship between implication and equivalence is fundamental to mathematical proof.

  • Equivalence of Implication and Contrapositive:
    \((A ⇒ B) ⇔ (¬B ⇒ ¬A)\)
    This means "If it rains, the street is wet" is logically equivalent to "If the street is not wet, it did not rain".
    Proving the contrapositive is often an easier way to prove an original statement.
  • Equivalence and Implication:
    \((A ⇔ B) ⇔ (A ⇒ B) ∧ (B ⇒ A)\)
    This is a vital proof strategy.
    To prove \(A\) and \(B\) are equivalent, you prove both
    \(A⇒ B\) (\(A\) is sufficient for \(B\)) and \(B ⇒ A\) (\(B\) is sufficient for \(A\), or \(A\) is necessary for \(B\))
