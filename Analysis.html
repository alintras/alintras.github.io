    • 1. Mathematical Logic and Set Theory

    1.1 Intro

    Calculus, which is short for infinitesimal calculus usually understood to mean differential and integral calculus of real and complex numbers.

    More generally, calculus may refer to any method or system of calculation guided by the sybolic manipulation of expressions.
    In that sense, calculus is the beginning part of the broader field of mathematical analysis, the section of mathematics concerned with the notion of a limit and limits of functions.

    In modern mathematics, the objects under investigation are almost always sets.
    So one aims at deriving true statements about sets from other statements about sets known or assumed to be true.
    Such a derivation or proof means applying logical rules that guarantee the truth of the derived statement.

    1.2 Propositional Calculus

    Statements

    By a statement or proposition we mean any sentence (any sequence of symbols) that can reasonably be assigned a truth value.
    i.e. a value of either true, abbreviated T, or false, abbreviated F.

    Sentences that are statements:

    • Every dog is an animal. (T)
    • Every animal is a dog. (F)
    • The number 4 is odd. (F)
    • 2 + 3 = 5. (T)
    • âˆš2 < 0. (F)
    • x + 1 > 0 holds for each natural number x. (T)

    Sentences that are not statements:

    • Let's study calculus!
    • Who are you?
    • 3 * 5 + 7.
    • x + 1 > 0.
    • All natural numbers are green.

    x + 1 > 0 is not a statement as it can not be said to be either true or false without further knowledge on x.
    "All natural numbers are green" is not a statement as it lacks any meaning. It would become a statement if give a definition of what it means for a natural number to be green.
