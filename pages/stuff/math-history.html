Historical Introduction to Maths

Early history in Babylon (modern-day Iraq) and Egypt:

Babylonians (around 2000 BCE): Systems of equations were solved approximately.
With a,b given, the goal was to find x,y:

\[ \left. \begin{aligned} x + y &= a \\ x \cdot y &= b \end{aligned} \right\} \implies x^2 - a x + b = 0 \]

This corresponds to solving quadratic equations, but with restrictions:
no irrational numbers, no negative numbers, only first approximations.

Egyptians (around 1700 BCE):

Linear equations of the form:

\[x + a \cdot x = b\] \[x + c \cdot x + d \cdot x = e\]

(with a,b,c,d,e given, solving for x) were solved using geometric methods.

Peak of development in ancient Greece:

Pythagoreans (around 600 BCE):

Discovery of incommensurable lengths and therefore irrational numbers by Hippasus of Metapontum.

Euclid (around 300 BCE):

Wrote the 13 books of the Elements.
This remained the standard work in geometry and arithmetic until the 19th century (and was the second most widely distributed book after the Bible).
Euclid is credited with proving the irrationality of √2.

Diophantus (between 100 BCE and 300 CE):

Wrote the 13 books of Arithmetica, of which only six survive.
He separated arithmetic and algebra from geometry.

Further development:

Mathematics advanced in the Arab-Persian and Indian worlds, peaking in the 7th–9th centuries.

Modern development:

It was only during the Renaissance in 16th-century Italy that mathematics (and all other sciences) developed further in Europe.
From the 18th century onwards, influential mathematicians such as Euler, Lagrange, Gauss, Galois, Abel, Jordan, Hölder, Lie, Killing, Cartan, Grassmann, Hamilton, Clifford, Cauchy, Ricci and Levi-Civita shaped its further development.
