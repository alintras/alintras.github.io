1.1 Euclidean Mathematics

In ancient Greece (represented partcularly by Euclid), numbers and arithmetic operations were based on geometric intuition.
Mathematical proofs were carried out using geometry.
We will consider a proof of the irrationality of √2.

1.1.1 Definition of Incommensurability:

Two line segments are called incommensurable if the have no common measure.
They are called commensurable if they share a common measure.
If two line segments have a common measure, then they have a greates common measure, which is unique.
Proof: antanairesis / Euclidean algorithm

1.1.2 Definition of GCD:

The greatest common measure of two natural numbers is the GCD (greatest common divisor).
The Euclidean Algorithm for constructing the greatest common measure is the so-called antanairesis (method of reciprocal or succesive subtraction).

Let X and Y be two line segments.
Without loss of generality, let Y ≤ X.
To find their common measure (or for a natural number X, Y, their GCD),
we repeatedly subtract Y from X until a remainder r1 remains, 0 ≤ r1 < Y.

If r1 = 0, then Y = gcd(X,Y).
If r1 > 0, subtract r1 from Y until a remainder r2 remains, 0 ≤ r2 < Y.
If r2 = 0, then r1 = gcd(X,Y).
If r2 > 0, subtract r2 from r1 until a remainder r3 remains, and so on.

Thus, we obtain the following system of equations (with ni ∈ ℕ for all i = 0,...,k and Y = r0):

X = n0Y + r1
Y = n1r1 + r2
r1 = n2r2 + r3
⋮
rk-1 = nkrk + rk+1

If rk+1 = 0 ⇒ rk = gcd(X,Y)

The process of successive subtraction provides a unique characterization of the ratio X/Y through the natural numbers n0, n1, ..., nk:

$$\frac{X}{Y} = n_0 + \frac{r_1}{Y} = n_0 + \frac{1}{\frac{Y}{r_1}} = n_0 + \frac{1}{n_1 + \frac{r_2}{r_1}} = n_0 + \frac{1}{n_1 + \frac{1}{n_2 + \frac{r_3}{r_2}}} = ... = n_0 + \frac{1}{n_1 + \frac{1}{n_2 + \frac{1}{... + \frac{1}{n_k}}}}$$

If the continued fraction terminates, X and Y are commensurable.
If it does not, they are incommensurable.

1.1.3 Definition of Irrational Numbers:

The ratio of two incommensurable line segments is called and irrational number,
the ratio of two commensurable line segments is called a rational number.

Example: Continued fraction representation of \(\frac{5}{3}\) and \(\gcd(5, 3)\):

$$5 = 1 \cdot 3 + 2$$ $$3 = 1 \cdot 2 + 1$$ $$2 = 2 \cdot 2 + 0$$
⇒ \(\frac{5}{3} = 1 + \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1}{2}}\)

Next, we consider the proof of the incommensurability of the side and diagonal of a square.
From this follows the irrationality of √2.

1.1.4 Theorem of Diagonal of a Square:

The side and diagonal of a square are incommensurable.

Proof:

  1. Consider a square with side length \( a \) and diagonal \( d \).
  2. Place the side \( a \) along the diagonal \( d \).
    A small remainder \( a_1 \) remains, such that \( d = a + a_1 \).
  3. Now using this smaller segment \( a_1 \), construct another square whose diagonal is \( d_1 \).
    When placing \(a_1\) along \(d_1\), a new remainder \(a_2\) appears, satisfying \( a = 2a_1 + a_2 \).
  4. This process can be repeated indefinitely, each time producing a smaller remainder (\(a_2\), \(a_3\), ...).
Each step creates a new, smaller square, and the subtraction never ends. Therefore, the side and diagonal are incommensurable.
