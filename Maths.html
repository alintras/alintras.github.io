The task of mathematics is to establish truth or falsehood of (formalizable) statements using rigorous logic, and to provide methods for the solution of classes of problems.
Ideally, including rigorous proofs verifiying the validity to the methods.

1.1 Historical Introduction

Early history in Babylon (modern-day Iraq) and Egypt:

Babylonians (around 2000 BCE): Systems of equations were solved approximately.
With a,b given, the goal was to find x,y:

\[ \left. \begin{aligned} x + y &= a \\ x \cdot y &= b \end{aligned} \right\} \implies x^2 - a x + b = 0 \]

This corresponds to solving quadratic equations, but with restrictions:
no irrational numbers, no negative numvers, only first approximations.

Egyptians (around 1700 BCE):

Linear equations of the form:

\[ \begin{aligned} x + a \cdot x &= b \end{aligned} \] \[ \begin{aligned} x + c \cdot x + d \cdot x &= e \end{aligned} \]

(with a,b,c,d,e given, solving for x) were solved using geometric methods.

Peak of developement in ancient Greece:

Pythagoreans (around 600 BCE):

Discovery of incommensurable lengths and therefore irrational numbers by Hippasus of Metapontum.

Euclid (around 300 BCE):

Wrote the 13 books of the Elements.
This remained the standard work in geometry and arithmetic until the 19th century (and was the second most widely distributed book after the Bible).
Euclid is credited with proving the irrationality of √2.

Diophantus (between 100 BCE and 300 CE):

Wrote the 13 books of Arithmetica, of which only six survive
He seperated arithmetic and algebra from geometry.

Further development:

Mathematics advanced in the Arab-Persian and Indian worlds, peaking in the 7th - 9th centuries.

Modern developement:

It was only during the Renaissance in 16th-century Italy that mathematics (and all other sciences) developed further in Europe.
From the 18th centuryonwards , influential mathematicians such as Euler, Lagrange, Gauss, Galois, Abel, Jordan Hölder, Lie, Killing, Carta, Grassmann, Hamilton, Clifford, Cauchy, Ricci and Levi-Civita shaped its further developement.

1.2 Euclidean Mathematics

In ancient Greece (represented partcularly by Euclid), numbers and arithmetic operations were based on geometric intuition.
Mathematical proofs were carried out using geometry.
We will consider a proof of the irrationality of √2.

1.2.1 Definition of Incommensurability:

Two line segments are called incommensurable if the have no common measure.
They are called commensurable if they share a common measure.
If two line segments have a common measure, then they have a greates common measure, which is unique.
Proof: antanairesis / Euclidean algorithm

1.2.2 Definition of GCD:

The greatest common measure of two natural numbers is the GCD (greatest common divisor).
The Euclidean Algorithm for constructing the greatest common measure is the so-called antanairesis (method of reciprocal or succesive subtraction).

Let X and Y be two line segments.
Without loss of generality, let Y ≤ X.
To find their common measure (or for a natural number X, Y, their GCD),
we repeatedly subtract Y from X until a remainder r1 remains, 0 ≤ r1 < Y.

If r1 = 0, then Y = gcd(X,Y).
If r1 > 0, subtract r1 from Y until a remainder r2 remains, 0 ≤ r2 < Y.
If r2 = 0, then r1 = gcd(X,Y).
If r2 > 0, subtract r2 from r1 until a remainder r3 remains, and so on.

Thus, we obtain the following system of equations (with ni ∈ ℕ for all i = 0,...,k and Y = r0):

X = n0Y + r1
Y = n1r1 + r2
r1 = n2r2 + r3
⋮
rk-1 = nkrk + rk+1

If rk+1 = 0 ⇒ rk = gcd(X,Y)

The process of successive subtraction provides a unique characterization of the ratio X/Y through the natural numbers n0, n1, ..., nk:

$$\frac{X}{Y} = n_0 + \frac{r_1}{Y} = n_0 + \frac{1}{\frac{Y}{r_1}} = n_0 + \frac{1}{n_1 + \frac{r_2}{r_1}} = n_0 + \frac{1}{n_1 + \frac{1}{n_2 + \frac{r_3}{r_2}}} = ... = n_0 + \frac{1}{n_1 + \frac{1}{n_2 + \frac{1}{... + \frac{1}{n_k}}}}$$

If the continued fraction terminates, X and Y are commensurable.
If it does not, they are incommensurable.

1.2.3 Definition of Irrational Numbers:

The ratio of two incommensurable line segments is called and irrational number,
the ratio of two commensurable line segments is called a rational number.

Example: Continued fraction representation of \(\frac{5}{3}\) and \(\gcd(5, 3)\):

$$5 = 1 \cdot 3 + 2$$ $$3 = 1 \cdot 2 + 1$$ $$2 = 2 \cdot 2 + 0$$
⇒ \(\frac{5}{3} = 1 + \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1}{2}}\)

Next, we consider the proof of the incommensurability of the side and diagonal of a square.
From this follows the irrationality of √2.

1.2.4 Theorem of Diagonal of a Square:

The side and diagonal of a square are incommensurable.

Proof:

  1. Start with a square of side \( a \) and diagonal \( d \).
  2. Place \( a \) along \( d \);
    a small remainder \( a_1 \) remains, so \( d = a + a_1 \).
  3. This leftover \( a_1 \) forms the side of a smaller square with diagonal \( d_1 \), where \( a = a_1 + d_1 \).
  4. Repeating the process — placing \( a_1 \) along \( d_1 \) — leaves another remainder \( a_2 \), so \( a = 2a_1 + a_2 \).
Each step creates a new, smaller square, and the subtraction never ends. Therefore, the side and diagonal are incommensurable.

1. Mathematical Logic and Set Theory

1.1 Propositional Calculus

Statements

By a statement or proposition we mean any sentence (any sequence of symbols) that can reasonably be assigned a truth value.
i.e. a value of either true, abbreviated T, or false, abbreviated F.

Sentences that are statements:

  • Every dog is an animal. (T)
  • Every animal is a dog. (F)
  • The number 4 is odd. (F)
  • 2 + 3 = 5. (T)
  • √2 < 0. (F)
  • x + 1 > 0 holds for each natural number x. (T)

Sentences that are not statements:

  • Let's study calculus!
  • Who are you?
  • 3 * 5 + 7.
  • x + 1 > 0.
  • All natural numbers are green.

x + 1 > 0 is not a statement as it can not be said to be either true or false without further knowledge on x.
"All natural numbers are green" is not a statement as it lacks any meaning. It would become a statement if give a definition of what it means for a natural number to be green.
